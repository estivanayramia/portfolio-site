Background

I was born in Baghdad, Iraq. My family fled due to war, first going to Syria as refugees before being sponsored to come to the United States. After about 5 to 6 years, we earned US citizenship. I have lived in El Cajon, California since I was about 4 years old.

I am Chaldean, part of an ancient community that traces back to Mesopotamian times. We speak Neo-Aramaic, a dialect related to the language Jesus spoke. Growing up between two worlds - Middle Eastern heritage at home, American culture outside - taught adaptability, cultural sensitivity, and the importance of bridging divides.

My parents had an arranged marriage. My dad was an electrical engineer. My mom restarted her education in the United States and earned a Bachelor of Science in Business Administration from SDSU after around 10 years of schooling. I am the youngest of four brothers: Alen (oldest), Andrew, Evan, and then me.

The Chaldean experience is rooted in survival and reinvention. Ancestors fled persecution, rebuilt lives in new countries, and passed down a relentless work ethic. That history shapes perspective: when your family's story involves losing everything and starting over, you learn not to take opportunities for granted.

First-generation trajectory means navigating systems that weren't built with you in mind. No family connections in corporate America, no inherited wealth, no safety net. That builds resourcefulness, self-reliance, and a deep appreciation for mentorship.

This background isn't a sob story - it's an advantage. It creates hunger, adaptability, and a refusal to settle for mediocrity. It's why the focus is on building systems that scale, documenting everything, and creating value that speaks louder than credentials.

Cognitive edge: I test in roughly the top 2 percent for pattern recognition, which helps with systems thinking and spotting connections others might miss.

Make allies, not enemies

Why this matters to me: In every conflict I have seen, the people who win long-term are the ones who build bridges instead of burning them. It is slower, but the relationships compound.

Why this matters to me: Everyone is fighting battles you cannot see. When someone frustrates me, I try to remember I am only seeing one moment in their entire life.
