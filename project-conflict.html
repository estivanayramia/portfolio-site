Grimes Interview

Deep-dive Q&A exploring AI ethics in art, creative independence from major labels, and the future of decentralized music production with one of the industry's most forward-thinking artists.

Overview

This interview was conducted with Isa Grimes, the former CMO of Create Music Group, one of the largest independent music distribution and marketing companies in the industry. The goal was to understand how marketing leadership operates at scale in the music business.

What started as a simple informational interview became a deep conversation about career strategy, leadership frameworks, and what actually matters when building a marketing function from the ground up.

Context

Create Music Group has distributed over 10 million tracks and works with some of the biggest independent artists and labels in the world. As CMO, Isa was responsible for the entire marketing function, including brand partnerships, artist marketing, and internal team development.

I reached out cold on LinkedIn. No mutual connections. Just a genuine message explaining what I was trying to learn. She responded within 24 hours.

Key Questions and Responses

Q: What does a CMO actually do day-to-day at a music company?

"Most people think marketing is just campaigns. At this level, it's about systems. Building the team, creating processes that scale, making sure every touchpoint with an artist or partner reflects the brand. Day-to-day is a lot of meetings, a lot of reviewing work, a lot of removing obstacles for my team."

Q: How did you get to this level? What did your career path look like?

"I started in artist management, doing everything myself. Social media, booking, strategy, all of it. That forced me to learn every piece of the puzzle. Then I moved agency-side, then in-house at a label, and eventually here. Each step taught me something different about how the industry actually works."

Q: What separates good marketing people from great ones?

"Great ones obsess over the details but never lose sight of the big picture. They can zoom in and out. And they're relentless about results. Not just activity, but actual outcomes. A lot of marketing people are busy. The great ones are effective."

Q: What advice would you give someone trying to break into music marketing?

"Pick an artist you believe in and just start doing the work. Don't wait for permission. Build case studies. Show what you can do. The industry is smaller than you think. Good work gets noticed."

Q: What's the biggest mistake you see young people make?

"Focusing on title and company name instead of learning. Your first few years should be about absorbing everything. Work for someone who will teach you. Take the role that gives you more responsibility, not the one with the better-sounding title."

What I Learned

  • Systems over campaigns: Marketing leadership is about building repeatable processes, not just executing one-off ideas
  • Generalist first: Learning every piece of the puzzle early gives you perspective that specialists lack
  • Outcomes over activity: Being busy means nothing. Impact is what matters
  • Don't wait for permission: Start doing the work before anyone gives you the official role
  • Cold outreach works: A genuine, specific message to the right person can open doors

Why This Matters

This conversation crystallized a lot of things I'd been thinking about. The importance of learning by doing, not waiting for permission. The value of understanding systems, not just executing tasks. The power of reaching out to people you want to learn from.

It also showed me that the people at the top are more accessible than most people assume. A well-crafted message to someone who sees potential in you can change your trajectory.

What I Learned About Myself

This project taught me something about how I operate best: I do well when I prepare obsessively and then let the conversation flow naturally. I spent hours researching Isa before the call, but during the call I let go of my prepared questions and just listened.

I also learned that I am more comfortable reaching out to strangers than I thought. The fear of rejection is smaller than the regret of not trying. That realization has changed how I approach networking since.

Finally, I confirmed that I learn better from conversations than from reading. There is something about hearing someone explain their thinking that sticks with me in a way that books do not.
